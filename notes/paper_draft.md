# Related Work

## Paragraph 1 — Foundations and Problem Definition

Shu et al. [#1] frame fake news detection as a data mining problem and organize the field around two feature families — news content (linguistic and visual signals from the article itself) and social context (user, post, and network signals from how the article spreads) — with detection models further divided into knowledge-based, style-based, stance-based, and propagation-based approaches. In parallel, Vlachos and Riedel [#2] formalize the alternative paradigm of computational fact-checking, defining it as the assignment of a truth value to a contextualized claim and decomposing the task into a four-stage pipeline — claim extraction, question construction, evidence retrieval, and verdict prediction — that anchors the claim-evidence-verdict structure used in subsequent verification work. An even earlier typology from Conroy, Rubin and Chen [#3] separates linguistic-cue methods (which analyze message content for deception markers) from network-based methods (including knowledge-graph fact-checking) — an early precedent for the detection-versus-verification framing this paper develops. More recently, surveys have consolidated each paradigm in parallel. Zhou and Zafarani [#4] organize fake news detection along four perspectives — knowledge, style, propagation, and source — explicitly separating evidence-based fact-checking from style-based content analysis. The corresponding treatment of automated fact-checking comes from Guo et al. [#5], who organize it into a three-stage pipeline of claim detection, evidence retrieval, and claim verification, with the latter further decomposing into verdict prediction and justification production. Yet direct empirical comparisons between the two paradigms — on the same claims, using the same evaluation — remain rare in this literature, despite the maturity of each side individually.

## Paragraph 2 — Bridging the Two Paradigms

Karadzhov et al. [#6] work directly at the seam: given a claim, their system generates a short tf-idf-weighted query, retrieves snippets and full pages from commercial search engines, and feeds claim-evidence similarity features together with LSTM-encoded representations of the claim and retrieved text into a hybrid SVM+neural classifier — combining content-level features (the detection paradigm's natural input) with retrieved external evidence (the verification paradigm's natural input) inside a single end-to-end system. Bondielli and Marcelloni [#7] take a complementary survey-level view, arguing that fake news detection and rumour detection are "strongly connected" aspects of the same general problem and unifying them into a single taxonomy of content-based and context-based features — yet within this unified frame, computational fact-checking appears only briefly, grouped under "other approaches" alongside anomaly detection and Hawkes-process modelling, rather than as a paradigm of comparable weight to content-based detection. What is missing from both lines of work, then, is not a recognition that the two paradigms exist, but a direct empirical comparison on aligned claims — one that asks where detection's surface-pattern reasoning succeeds, where evidence-based verification succeeds, and where each fails on the same inputs. This paper provides that comparison.

## Paragraph 3 — Detection via Linguistic & Stylistic Features

Pérez-Rosas et al. [#8] show that purely linguistic features — n-grams, punctuation, readability metrics, LIWC psycholinguistic categories, and CFG-based syntactic features — combined with a linear SVM reach 74-76% accuracy on two balanced fake news datasets spanning six general-news domains and a separate celebrity-news domain, well above the 50% chance baseline and comparable to untrained human annotators. Rashkin et al. [#9] sharpen this picture by quantifying which specific linguistic markers separate trusted from unreliable news across a graded truthfulness spectrum: unreliable sources use significantly more subjective language, hedging, modal and manner adverbs, superlatives, and second-person pronouns, while trusted sources lean on concrete figures — numbers, money references, and comparatives — and more assertive verbs, a pattern they confirm with statistical significance on both a four-class news-type corpus and a six-point PolitiFact truthfulness scale. The cleanest classical-pipeline benchmark comes from Ahmed, Traore and Saad [#10], who compare six classifiers (Linear SVM, SVM, SGD, Logistic Regression, KNN, Decision Tree) crossed with two feature schemes (raw term frequency and TF-IDF) and four n-gram orders on three datasets, and find that TF-IDF with unigrams or bigrams fed to a linear classifier — Linear SVM in particular — yields 90% accuracy on the Ott et al. deceptive-reviews benchmark and 92% accuracy on a new 2016 US-election news dataset, with linear models consistently outperforming non-linear ones and TF-IDF consistently outperforming raw counts across nearly every configuration. Taken together, these results establish a clear empirical picture for the detection paradigm: linguistic and stylistic features paired with TF-IDF and a linear classifier reliably achieve roughly 75-92% accuracy within a fixed domain, while their authors themselves repeatedly flag that this performance depends on a controlled training setting and degrades when the test domain or source shifts — motivating the TF-IDF-plus-linear-classifier baseline used in this study as a classical anchor for the detection paradigm.

## Paragraph 4 — Detection via Transformers

Devlin et al. [#11] introduce BERT, a deeply bidirectional transformer pre-trained on large unlabeled corpora through masked language modelling and next-sentence prediction, and demonstrate that the same pre-trained model can be fine-tuned with only a single added classification layer to achieve state-of-the-art results across eleven natural language understanding tasks — including text classification, which is the formulation fake news detection inherits. Within fake news specifically, Reis et al. [#12] provide the closest classical comparison, benchmarking five classifiers — KNN, Naive Bayes, Random Forest, RBF-SVM, and XGBoost — over a consolidated 170-feature set spanning textual, source-credibility, and social-engagement signals on the BuzzFace dataset of 2,282 labeled 2016 US-election articles, and find that tree ensembles (Random Forest and XGBoost) reach 0.85-0.86 AUC and 0.81 Macro-F1, statistically tied at the top, with a Chi-Square ranking that places source-credibility and engagement features above textual ones in discriminative power. Together these results justify the second detection arm in this study — a fine-tuned transformer detector paired with the TF-IDF baseline as two representatives of the same paradigm — while Reis et al.'s finding that non-textual features outrank textual ones quietly foreshadows the broader question of what any text-only detector is actually learning.

## Paragraph 5 — Detection Benchmarks

Wang [#13] introduces LIAR, a dataset of 12,836 short political statements (average length 17.9 tokens) collected from PolitiFact over 2007–2016, each labelled by a PolitiFact editor along a six-point truthfulness scale — pants-fire, false, barely-true, half-true, mostly-true, true — and accompanied by speaker metadata (party, job, state, context/venue, and a credit-history vector of the speaker's prior label counts), making it an order of magnitude larger than the earlier PolitiFact-derived resources released by Vlachos and Riedel [#2]. The task itself is hard: against a 0.208 majority-class baseline, Wang's strongest text-only model — a CNN over word2vec embeddings — reaches only 0.270 test accuracy on the six-way classification, and a hybrid CNN that fuses the full metadata bundle with text improves this by just 0.4 points to 0.274, indicating that surface-level content offers a low ceiling for fine-grained truthfulness classification on isolated short statements. This study uses LIAR as its primary detection dataset, collapsing the six-way labels into a binary fake-versus-real split — following the precedent Rashkin et al. [#9] set in collapsing PolitiFact's graded scale for binary evaluation — and trading graded sensitivity for cross-paradigm comparability with the binary verification benchmarks described in Paragraph 9.

## Paragraph 6 — The Limit of Detection: Style ≠ Truth ⭐ THESIS HINGE

The detection results surveyed above — whether from classical linear classifiers on TF-IDF features or from fine-tuned transformers — establish that supervised models can separate "real" from "fake" articles at 80-92% accuracy within a fixed dataset, but a separate line of work asks a sharper question: when a stylistic detector succeeds, what is it actually learning to recognise? Schuster et al. [#14] sharpen this question by separating two tasks for the same state-of-the-art stylometric detector — distinguishing machine-generated from human-written text (provenance) and distinguishing false from truthful machine-generated text (veracity) — and finding a striking asymmetry: the classifier flags even a single machine-generated sentence inside an otherwise human-written article with 0.94 F1, yet on text that is entirely machine-generated it cannot tell true content from false content above chance (0.53 F1 on negation-flipped articles, 0.71 F1 on a question-answering benchmark where over half the apparent signal is a length confound), supporting their core claim that whereas humans diverge stylistically when lying, language models generate stylistically consistent text regardless of underlying intent. A complementary demonstration on human-written political news comes from Potthast et al. [#15]: training a stylometric classifier on a professionally fact-checked corpus of 1,627 articles from nine publishers — under a three-fold cross-validation that explicitly holds out one publisher per orientation so the model cannot win by learning outlet style — they find that the _same_ feature set distinguishes hyperpartisan from mainstream news at F1 = 0.78 and satire from real news at F1 = 0.81, yet collapses to F1 = 0.46 on the fake-versus-real task within hyperpartisan articles, failing to beat even the all-real majority baseline; style, in their summary, identifies _posture_ (whether an article is one-sided or detached, satirical or earnest) but not _veracity_ within a given posture. Across two regimes of misinformation — Schuster's machine-generated case and Potthast's human-written political case — the same structural limit appears, one already pre-shadowed by the empirical fragility findings of Paragraphs 3 and 4 (Pérez-Rosas et al.'s collapse from 0.78 to 0.48 accuracy across domains [#8], Rashkin et al.'s drop from 0.91 to 0.65 F1 across news sources [#9], and Wang's 0.27 ceiling on six-way short-statement classification [#13]): a detector trained on surface features learns the _style_ of fakeness — which is dataset-specific, generator-specific, and posture-specific — not its _factual content_, which is what actually determines whether a claim is true. Both demonstrations point at the same alternative: Schuster et al.'s human evaluation [#14] shows annotators jumping from 0.68 to 0.84 F1 on the same veracity task once granted access to external sources — a sixteen-point gain that came not from sharper stylistic intuition but from the ability to check claims against evidence — and Potthast et al. [#15] conclude that style-based detection is best positioned as a pre-filter directing human fact-checkers to the articles most likely to warrant verification; both, in other words, locate the answer to detection's structural limit in the paradigm the remainder of this section turns to.

## Paragraph 7 — Verification via NLI / Textual Entailment

Hanselowski et al. [#16] operationalize claim verification as multi-sentence textual entailment in their top-three FEVER 2018 system: given a claim and a set of evidence sentences retrieved from Wikipedia, they run an Enhanced Sequential Inference Model independently over each (claim, evidence sentence) pair, then combine the five resulting representations through a claim-conditioned attention mechanism before a three-layer classifier predicts one of Supported, Refuted, or NotEnoughInfo — the same three-way verdict structure that Vlachos and Riedel [#2] originally proposed and that Guo et al. [#5] frame as the canonical claim-verification stage. Bowman et al. [#17] established this task at scale with SNLI, a corpus of 570,152 human-written premise-hypothesis pairs labelled entailment, contradiction, or neutral — two orders of magnitude larger than the RTE challenge sets that preceded it — and showed that on a corpus of this size, both lexicalised classifiers and LSTM-based sentence-embedding models reach roughly 77-78% three-way accuracy, with the SNLI-trained representations transferring usefully to the SICK entailment task; the practical consequence for downstream work is that pretrained NLI models, trained on SNLI and its successors, are now available off the shelf as drop-in three-way entailment classifiers. Williams, Nangia and Bowman [#18] extend this setup with MultiNLI, a 433,000-pair corpus that preserves SNLI's three-way labelling scheme and crowdsourcing protocol but draws premises from ten written and spoken English genres rather than image captions alone — and partitions the genres so that five appear in training, development, and test ("matched") while the remaining five appear only at evaluation ("mismatched"), building a cross-domain transfer test directly into the corpus; the practical result for downstream verification work is that a single-genre CBOW model trained only on SNLI underperforms any MultiNLI-trained model on every MultiNLI genre, while MultiNLI-trained models reach comparable accuracy on matched and mismatched genres (72.3 and 72.1 ESIM accuracy respectively) — meaning a model trained on MultiNLI carries an NLI capability that travels across heterogeneous text, which is precisely the regime verification operates in. Taken together, these three results define the verification arm of this study: claim verification is framed as multi-sentence textual entailment in the style of Hanselowski et al. [#16], and the entailment classifier is a pretrained NLI model whose representations were learned on the SNLI [#17] and MultiNLI [#18] training data — used off the shelf, without further fine-tuning on FEVER, so that the verification arm tests how far a generic NLI capability transfers to the fact-verification setting rather than how well a model can be tuned to one benchmark, with the caveat that the three-way NLI label space (entailment, contradiction, neutral) maps only approximately onto FEVER's Supported, Refuted, and NotEnoughInfo verdicts — a mismatch this paper inherits and revisits in its error analysis.

## Paragraph 8 — Verification Pipelines and Architectures

Nie, Chen and Bansal [#19] formalize the canonical FEVER architecture as three connected stages tackled by a single homogeneous neural semantic matching network (NSMN): a document-retrieval stage that narrows roughly five million Wikipedia pages to a handful of candidates by combining keyword matching with NSMN-based reranking over ambiguous titles, a sentence-selection stage that scores every sentence in the retrieved pages against the claim and keeps the top five, and a verification stage that concatenates the selected evidence into a single premise, pairs it with the claim, and predicts one of Supported, Refuted, or NotEnoughInfo — augmenting the standard NLI inputs with WordNet ontological features, dedicated number embeddings, and the upstream retrieval confidence scores themselves, so that the verifier sees how strongly its evidence was endorsed by the retrieval modules. Their UNC-NLP system reached 64.23 FEVER score on the FEVER 1.0 blind test (label accuracy 68.16, evidence F1 52.81), narrowly taking first place over the sibling top-three systems of Hanselowski et al. [#16] and Yoneda et al., and establishing the three-stage retrieval-then-entailment shape that subsequent FEVER work has largely retained. Upstream of the retrieval-and-entailment cascade Nie et al. describe sits an implicit stage-zero — deciding which sentences in a stream of discourse are worth checking at all — which Hassan et al. [#20] formalize as supervised three-way classification (non-factual sentence / unimportant factual sentence / check-worthy factual sentence) trained on a crowdsourced corpus of 20,617 sentences from US presidential debates spanning 1960–2012, and operationalize as the ClaimBuster scoring system: an SVM over tf-idf words, POS-tag counts, and entity-type counts that achieves precision 0.72 / recall 0.67 on the check-worthy class, P@100 = 0.96 against high-quality human coders, and — most importantly for the pipeline framing — produces check-worthiness scores that are statistically far higher for sentences subsequently fact-checked by CNN (mean 0.433 vs 0.258, t = 21.1) and PolitiFact (mean 0.438 vs 0.258, t = 16.4) than for sentences left unchecked, evidence that an automated claim-selection stage learnably mirrors what professional fact-checkers actually choose to verify. Taken together, these two stages — Hassan-style claim selection upstream and Nie-style retrieval-then-entailment downstream — frame the real-world fact-checking pipeline as a sequence of imperfect filters, each of which loses headroom (Hanselowski's full FEVER pipeline degrades 93.55 × 87.10 × 68.49 to a 64.74 full-pipeline score); to study the verification step itself rather than the cascade around it, this paper takes FEVER claims and their gold evidence sentences as given, treating both the claim-selection and document-retrieval stages as solved, so that the comparison between detection and verification turns on entailment reasoning rather than on retrieval engineering or claim-worthiness scoring.

## Paragraph 9 — Verification Benchmarks

Thorne et al. [#21] introduce FEVER, a dataset of 185,445 claims constructed by having annotators extract single-fact claims from ~50,000 popular Wikipedia pages and then mutate them along six Natural-Logic-Inference-inspired axes (paraphrase, negation, entity/relation substitution, generalisation, specification), with a separate annotation pass labelling each claim Supported, Refuted, or NotEnoughInfo and selecting the evidence sentences — annotators in this second pass aware of the originating page but not the originating sentence, which lifts the task from trivial paraphrase-detection into one where 31.75% of claims admit multiple valid evidence sets, 16.82% require composition across sentences, and 12.15% require evidence from multiple pages. The dataset achieves Fleiss κ = 0.6841 on three-way labelling and 95.42% precision / 72.36% recall on evidence selection against super-annotators, and is partitioned into a 145,449-claim training set with class-imbalanced labels (S 80,035 / R 29,775 / NEI 35,639) and three balanced 9,999-claim development, test, and reserved sets — making FEVER three orders of magnitude larger than Vlachos and Riedel's 106-statement seed dataset [#2] and the de facto standard verification benchmark every system in this paragraph and the next is built on. At the opposite end of the controlled-versus-naturalistic axis sits MultiFC, where Augenstein et al. [#22] crawl 34,918 naturally occurring claims from 26 English fact-checking websites — PolitiFact, Snopes, FactCheck.org, AfricaCheck and others — pair each claim with rich journalistic metadata (speaker, checker, topic tags, linked Wikipedia entities) and ten Google Search evidence pages, and frame the resulting 165-label-space heterogeneity (ranging from two labels per domain to twenty-seven) as a multi-task learning problem with a shared label-embedding layer and a jointly learned evidence-ranking module trained end-to-end on veracity supervision alone; their best evidence-and-metadata model reaches only **49.2% Macro F1** averaged across the 26 domains — a striking ceiling compared to the ~65% three-way scores top systems achieve on FEVER [#21, #19, #16], and the load-bearing empirical evidence that real-world claims with heterogeneous web evidence and journalist-defined label schemes are substantially harder than mutation-generated Wikipedia claims with curated single-source evidence. A third axis of difficulty — orthogonal to FEVER's clean Wikipedia evidence and MultiFC's noisy web evidence — is **domain expertise**, which Wadden et al. [#23] foreground with **SciFact**, a dataset of 1,409 expert-written atomic scientific claims verified against a curated corpus of 5,183 biomedical abstracts with three-way labels (SUPPORTS / REFUTES / NOINFO) and sentence-level rationales annotated by life-science graduate students and NLP experts. SciFact's construction is the structural opposite of FEVER's annotator-mutation pipeline: claims are _re-written from naturally occurring citation sentences_ without showing annotators the cited abstract, REFUTES claims are hand-negated by an expert who avoids obvious cues like "not," and the resulting claim-only baseline reaches only 44.5% three-way accuracy (versus the ~60% Schuster et al. [#24] document on FEVER) — empirical evidence that careful claim construction can resist the shortcut-learning problems built into mutation-based benchmarks. Their pipeline baseline VERISCI — structurally identical to Nie's three-stage retrieve-select-label architecture [#19] but using RoBERTa-large encoders pretrained on FEVER and fine-tuned on SciFact — reaches **46.5% abstract-level Label+Rationale F1 in the open setting** but jumps to **72.7% F1 given gold abstracts** and 83.3% F1 given gold rationales, replicating on biomedicine the same retrieval-as-bottleneck pattern Hanselowski et al. [#16], Nie et al. [#19], and Thorne et al. [#21] document on FEVER. The five failure modes their error analysis identifies — science background, directionality, numerical reasoning, cause-and-effect, and coreference — overlap directly with the compound failure modes already foregrounded across [#9], [#16], [#19], and [#20], establishing that **the same reasoning capabilities are demanded across political news, encyclopedic facts, and scientific findings**, just over different vocabularies. Given this spectrum of difficulty — controlled Wikipedia claims, naturalistic journalistic claims, expert-written scientific claims — and given that three independent pipelines on two corpora locate most of the lost headroom in retrieval rather than in reasoning over correct evidence, this study takes the verification arm to FEVER with oracle (gold) evidence: not because that setup is realistic, but because it isolates the entailment-reasoning step that is the methodological counterpart to detection, holding both retrieval and claim-selection idealisations constant so the contrast in the results section turns on what each paradigm is actually being asked to do rather than on how robustly each pipeline stage performs in the wild — a choice that buys clarity at the price of acknowledging, in the next paragraph, that even this controlled setup has its own evaluation pathologies.

## Paragraph 10 — The Limit of Verification: Artifacts & Adversarial Fragility ⭐ MIRRORS PARAGRAPH 6

That pathology is documented most surgically by Schuster et al. [#24], who train a BERT classifier on FEVER claims alone — never showing it any evidence — and find it reaches **61.7% three-way accuracy** against a 33.3% majority baseline and only 8 percentage points behind the evidence-aware NSMN state-of-the-art [#19]; their Local Mutual Information analysis traces the bulk of this margin to systematic claim-side n-gram correlations introduced during FEVER's mutation-based annotation protocol [#21], with the top REFUTES-correlated bigrams in the training set — _did not_, _yet to_, _does not_, _refused to_, _failed to_, _incapable being_, _unable to_ — almost all expressing negation, the structural fingerprint of how annotators turned a Wikipedia sentence into a REFUTES claim. A complementary ablation with random rather than GloVe embeddings (54.1% vs 57.3% claim-only accuracy) rules out world knowledge as the explanation, locating the gap inside the dataset rather than inside the embeddings. The diagnostic counterpart to this measurement is a hand-constructed **Symmetric Test Set of 956 claim-evidence pairs**, in which each original FEVER pair is paired with a manually generated contradicting twin so that claim-only shortcuts are reduced to chance by construction: on this set, the same NSMN classifier that reaches 81.8% on FEVER dev drops to **58.7%**, ESIM drops from 80.8% to 55.9%, and BERT drops from 86.2% to 58.3% — a 23-to-28 point gap on identical vocabulary that quantifies how much of standard FEVER performance is artifact-driven rather than evidence-driven. The implication runs deeper than a single benchmark: a system that reaches high accuracy by reading the claim alone is not performing verification at all, regardless of what its label outputs look like, and any reported FEVER number is therefore best read as an upper bound on evidence-conditioned reasoning rather than as a direct measurement of it. The same shortcut phenomenon — different surface form, same underlying pattern — has been observed in natural language inference corpora [#17, #18] and in real-world fact-checking data with retrieved web evidence [#22], and the one benchmark in our reading list that resists it [#23] does so only through deliberate construction choices (citance-derived claims, hand-negation without lexical cues) that are absent from FEVER's mutation-based pipeline. Claim-side artifacts are thus a property of how verification benchmarks get built when one side of the input is human-authored under task constraints, not a property of FEVER specifically; this is the lens through which any verification result in this paper, including our own, should be read. The artifact diagnosis has a complementary finding on the robustness side. In the FEVER 2.0 shared task, Thorne et al. [#25] re-evaluated seven leading FEVER systems — covering BERT-based natural-language-inference architectures, pointer networks trained with reinforcement learning, and OpenIE-triple semantic-similarity pipelines — against ~500 hand-constructed adversarial claim-evidence pairs designed to require multi-hop, temporal, or compositional reasoning rather than single-sentence matching, and reported a universal collapse: against standard FEVER test scores of 57.4-68.5%, **no system exceeded 37.3% resilience on the adversarial set, a gap of roughly thirty points common across the architectural families**. Two attack types — TMLab's Paraphrase attack, which rewrites Wikipedia sentences in vocabulary borrowed from outside the evidence corpus, and NbAuzDrLqg's SubsetNum attack, which requires transitive numerical reasoning about geographic regions — drove the joint label-and-evidence FEVER score to **0.00%** across all systems; revealingly, the Paraphrase attack reached **43.06% label accuracy on those same instances**, meaning systems often selected the correct verdict but never identified the correct evidence sentence, a dissociation that standard label-accuracy reporting hides and that joint-metric reporting surfaces. The only fix-it submission [#25], which targeted multi-hop retrieval and date-arithmetic specifically, recovered just **+3.69 resilience points** — small, attack-specific, and limited to the failure modes it was designed against. Read together, the artifact and adversarial findings parallel the detection-side limits of Paragraph 6: each paradigm has one well-documented "the data carries shortcuts" failure mode and one well-documented "the model is brittle under shifted inputs" failure mode, with the specific surface forms differing (style-versus-truth confusion on the detection side, claim-only and adversarial-fragility on the verification side) but the structural shape repeating. Neither limit can be fully escaped by an off-the-shelf comparison in a single course-scale project, and this paper makes no claim to escape them; rather, it takes both critiques as the entry condition for the empirical work that follows. The contribution this study aims at is therefore not a winner between detection and verification, but a characterisation of where each paradigm's headline accuracy comes from and where it breaks — measured on aligned inputs, with error analysis on both arms — so that the comparison itself, rather than any single accuracy number, becomes the result. The remainder of the paper operationalises this position: Methodology details how each paradigm is set up on shared data with a deliberately minimal pipeline; Experiments report accuracy alongside the failure modes the surveyed literature flags as expected; Discussion reads the gap between the two through the lens of the limits established here.
